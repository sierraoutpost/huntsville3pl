# Photography & Imagery

Photography is the single biggest differentiator between a site that feels real and one that feels like a template. Most logistics websites use the same 20 stock photos — the handshake over a blurry warehouse, the aerial shot of a container ship, the woman in a hard hat pointing at a clipboard while smiling at no one. Those images instantly communicate "we bought a template and filled it in."

Sams 3PL sites use **real photography** — real facilities, real loading docks, real cities, real people where possible. When stock photography is necessary, it comes from curated sources that prioritize editorial quality over generic corporate staging. And AI-generated images are never used.

This file defines exactly what's allowed, what's not, and how to art-direct imagery for every page type.

---

## 1. The Non-Negotiables

From the Master Manual:
- **Non-negotiable #15:** Real photos only — no AI-generated images, ever.
- **Non-negotiable #16:** Every page has a real photograph hero.
- **Non-negotiable #3:** Real photographs, not stock illustrations.

These are absolute. There is no "well, we don't have a photo of the Nashville facility yet, so let's use Midjourney for now." If we don't have a real photo, we use a real stock photo from an approved source that honestly represents logistics work. We never fabricate imagery.

## 2. The Image Sourcing Hierarchy

When selecting images for any page, follow this priority order:

### 2.1 Tier 1 — Commissioned photography (best)

Original photography shot at or for a Sams 3PL facility. This is the gold standard:
- Facility exterior and interior.
- Loading docks with real trucks.
- Warehouse operations (picking, packing, staging, shipping).
- Team members (with their permission and a signed release).
- City skyline or landmark shots taken on-site.

Commissioned photos are unique to us, impossible for competitors to replicate, and carry the strongest E-E-A-T signal. Prioritize commissioning photography for the home page hero, the about page, and the primary service page hero of each city.

**Art direction for commissioned shoots:** see §5.

### 2.2 Tier 2 — Licensed editorial stock (strong)

When commissioned photography isn't available, use editorial-quality stock from these approved providers:

| Provider | Why approved | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Unsplash+** | High-quality editorial photography, real moments, diverse subjects | City skylines, lifestyle, general logistics, people |
| **Getty Images** | Deep catalog, editorial tier is strong, rights-managed options available | Specific logistics operations, industry verticals |
| **Stocksy** | Curated, anti-generic, editorial-first philosophy | People, workspaces, city character, environmental |
| **Offset (by Shutterstock)** | Offset collection is curated for higher quality than standard Shutterstock | Facilities, industrial, transportation |
| **Adobe Stock** | Broad catalog, Premium collection has editorial quality | Equipment, transportation, city scenes |
| **Death to Stock** | Anti-stock aesthetic, limited but intentional catalog | Atmospheric, mood, lifestyle |

**Providers that are NOT approved:**
- Standard Shutterstock (non-Offset). Too generic, too staged, too "stock."
- iStock / Getty's lower tiers. Same problem.
- Pexels / Pixabay free tiers. Quality is inconsistent and the same images appear on thousands of sites.
- Any provider whose top results for "warehouse" include a woman in a hard hat smiling at a clipboard. If that image appears in the first page of results, the provider's curation is not strong enough.

### 2.3 Tier 3 — Free editorial stock (acceptable)

Unsplash (free tier) is acceptable when:
- The specific image is genuinely high-quality and editorial in character (not the top 10 most-downloaded photos in a category).
- The image is not recognizably overused (search Google Images reverse-search to check).
- No better option exists at Tiers 1 or 2.

Pexels is a last resort and only when a specific image is genuinely strong. Never browse Pexels generically — search with intent and evaluate each image against the art direction in §4.

### 2.4 Never — AI-generated imagery

No Midjourney, DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, Firefly, or any other generative AI imagery. Reasons:

1. **E-E-A-T.** AI images are the opposite of "Experience" — they prove we've never been inside a warehouse.
2. **Legal risk.** AI image copyright and licensing are unresolved as of 2026. We don't ship legally ambiguous assets.
3. **Detection.** AI-generated images are increasingly detectable by both humans and automated tools. Being caught using them is a brand credibility hit.
4. **Principle.** Tyler's non-negotiable is clear: "Real photos only." This isn't a cost optimization — it's a quality standard.

## 3. Image Licensing

### 3.1 License types we use

- **Royalty-free (RF):** One-time purchase, unlimited use across all Sams 3PL sites and marketing materials. This is the standard for stock purchases. Getty, Adobe Stock, Stocksy, and Offset all offer RF licenses.
- **Rights-managed (RM):** Licensed for specific use, duration, and geography. More expensive but guarantees exclusivity. Consider RM for hero images on the highest-traffic city sites (Detroit, Houston) to prevent competitors from using the same photo.
- **Creative Commons (CC):** Free to use with attribution. Unsplash uses a custom license that doesn't require attribution but appreciates it. If using CC-licensed images, comply with the specific license terms (CC0, CC-BY, CC-BY-SA, etc.).
- **Commissioned (work-for-hire):** We own full rights. The photographer's contract must include a work-for-hire clause granting Sams 3PL Solutions all rights, all media, perpetual use.

### 3.2 License documentation

Maintain a spreadsheet or database of every licensed image used across the network:

| Image | Source | License Type | Purchase Date | Used On | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| detroit-dock-hero.webp | Getty #12345678 | RF Standard | 2026-04-01 | detroit3pl.com home hero | $299 |
| houston-skyline.webp | Unsplash+ | Unsplash+ License | 2026-03-15 | houston3pl.com home hero | Subscription |
| toledo-team.webp | Commissioned (J. Rivera) | Work-for-hire | 2026-02-10 | toledo3pl.com about | $1,200 |

This protects against accidental license violations and makes it easy to audit what we're using where.

## 4. Art Direction — What "Real" Looks Like

### 4.1 The Sams 3PL visual tone

Our images share a consistent visual language:

- **Warm, natural light.** Golden hour, overcast daylight, or well-balanced interior lighting. Never harsh flash, never the flat overhead fluorescent lighting of a security camera. Warehouse interiors should feel warm and operational, not cold and empty.
- **Real activity.** People (where present) are doing something — driving a forklift, scanning a barcode, coordinating on a dock, loading a pallet. Never posing. Never pointing at nothing while smiling.
- **Honest scale.** Show the facility at its actual scale. If it's 50,000 sq ft, don't shoot it to look like 500,000. If it's a single dock, don't imply it's a megahub. Honesty builds trust; exaggeration destroys it.
- **Industrial texture.** Concrete floors, steel beams, corrugated metal, painted dock bumpers, tire marks, caution tape, pallet wood. These textures are beautiful when photographed well. They signal "this is a real place where real work happens."
- **Color palette alignment.** Photos should harmonize with the brand palette. Brick red and petrol blue appear naturally in logistics environments — red dock bumpers, blue steel, blue trucks, red signage. Select and commission photos where these colors occur organically. Avoid photos dominated by colors that clash with our palette (neon green, hot pink, electric purple).
- **Depth of field.** Shallow depth of field (blurred background, sharp subject) for people and detail shots. Deep focus for facility-wide establishing shots. This isn't a hard rule — it's a guideline for visual variety that keeps pages from feeling like surveillance footage.

### 4.2 What to avoid (the "stock photo tells")

These details instantly communicate "generic stock photo" and must be avoided:

- **The clipboard point.** A person in a hard hat pointing at a clipboard while looking at the camera. This is the universal signal of "stock warehouse photo."
- **The empty warehouse.** A pristine, empty warehouse with perfect lighting and no activity. Real warehouses are messy, busy, and full of stuff.
- **The handshake.** Two people in business attire shaking hands with a warehouse blurry in the background. This is the "corporate merger" photo repurposed for logistics.
- **The overhead container shot.** An aerial view of a container ship or shipping yard. It's been used on every logistics site since 2008 and communicates nothing specific about our operations.
- **Matching hard hats.** A group of people in identical hard hats and safety vests, standing in a line, all smiling at the camera. This is a safety training brochure, not a website.
- **The truck on an empty highway.** A lone semi truck on a desert highway at sunset. Beautiful photo, completely generic, used by every trucking company and freight broker on the internet.
- **The globe.** Any graphic showing a globe with shipping routes or logistics connections drawn on it. This is the logistics industry's clip art.
- **Over-saturated colors.** Stock photos with punched-up saturation to look "vibrant." Real logistics environments have muted, industrial tones. Embrace that.

### 4.3 The reverse-search test

Before using any stock photo, reverse-search it on Google Images or TinEye. If the image appears on more than 5 other websites — especially competitor logistics sites — reject it and find an alternative. Unique images build brand identity; overused images dilute it.

## 5. Art Direction for Commissioned Shoots

When commissioning photography for a Sams 3PL facility, provide the photographer with this brief:

### 5.1 Shot list (minimum viable shoot)

1. **Facility exterior** — wide establishing shot showing the building, signage, and surrounding environment. Golden hour preferred. Include at least one vehicle (truck, trailer) at the dock to communicate activity.
2. **Loading dock active** — medium shot of freight being moved through dock doors. Forklift or pallet jack in motion. Show the dock bumpers, levelers, and height — these are the operational details that prove E-E-A-T.
3. **Warehouse interior (wide)** — full racking/storage view showing depth and scale. Lights on, product visible, aisles clear. This is the "here's how much space we have" shot.
4. **Warehouse interior (detail)** — close-up of hands scanning a barcode, a pallet being wrapped, a label being applied. Human touch in an industrial setting.
5. **Team at work** — 2–3 people coordinating naturally. NOT posed. The photographer should capture a real operational moment — a handoff, a conversation about a shipment, a check-in at a workstation.
6. **City context** — the facility with the city skyline, a local landmark, or a recognizable geographic feature visible. This is the "we're part of this city" shot that feeds `12-city-character-injection.md`.
7. **Detail textures** — pallet wood grain, dock bumper paint, concrete floor with forklift tire marks, rack labels, safety signage. These become section dividers, card backgrounds, and atmospheric accents.

### 5.2 Technical requirements for commissioned photos

- **Resolution:** Minimum 4000px on the long edge (sufficient for full-bleed hero at 2× on a 1600px layout).
- **Format:** Delivered as RAW + high-quality JPEG. We'll convert to AVIF/WebP in the Astro build pipeline.
- **Color profile:** sRGB (web standard). Not Adobe RGB, not ProPhoto.
- **Aspect ratio:** A mix of 16:9 (heroes), 4:3 (cards), and 1:1 (team headshots). Capture wide and crop in post if needed.
- **Model releases:** Signed for every identifiable person. Stored alongside the license documentation (§3.2).
- **Property releases:** Signed for the facility if required by the property owner.

### 5.3 Budget guidance

A professional half-day shoot (4 hours) at a single facility typically costs $800–$2,500 depending on the market and photographer. This yields 50–100 edited images — enough for an entire city site plus blog illustrations for months. This is one of the highest-ROI investments in the entire project.

## 6. Per-Page Image Requirements

### 6.1 Home page

- **Hero:** Full-bleed photograph of the city's facility or a signature city scene with logistics context. This is the single most important image on the site. Commissioned Tier 1 strongly preferred.
- **Services section:** One image per featured service card (3 images). Can be Tier 2 stock if Tier 1 isn't available.
- **City character block:** One city-specific image (skyline, landmark, or neighborhood scene). Must be identifiably from that city — not a generic "American city" photo.
- **About preview:** One team or facility image.
- **Total:** 5–7 images on the home page.

### 6.2 Service detail pages

- **Hero:** Photograph of that specific service being performed (e.g., cross-docking in action, fulfillment picking, racking storage). Commissioned Tier 1 preferred.
- **Process illustration:** 1–2 images showing the service process or facility areas dedicated to that service.
- **Total:** 2–3 images per service page.

### 6.3 About page

- **Hero:** Facility exterior or team photo.
- **Team section:** Individual or small-group photos of named team members. Commissioned Tier 1 only — stock photos of "business people" on the about page is a credibility killer.
- **Facility details:** Interior shots showing racking, docks, equipment.
- **Total:** 4–8 images.

### 6.4 Connect page

- **Hero:** A warm, inviting image — facility interior with good lighting, or a team member at a workstation. The goal is to make the visitor feel like they're reaching a real person, not a form bot.
- **Total:** 1 image.

### 6.5 Blog posts

- **Hero:** One featured image per post. Must be relevant to the post's topic. Tier 1 or 2.
- **In-body images:** Optional, 1–3 per post where they add value (process diagrams, facility photos relevant to the topic, data visualizations).
- **Author headshot:** Used in the author bio block. Commissioned Tier 1 only for real team members; for editorial personas (see `10-blog-system.md`), use a consistent illustrated avatar or omit the photo entirely — never a stock headshot pretending to be a person who doesn't exist.
- **Total:** 1–4 images per post.

### 6.6 404 page

- **One image** — this can be the most creative image on the site. An unexpected angle of the facility, a detail shot that becomes charming out of context, or a city scene that rewards the visitor for stumbling off the path. This is an Easter egg candidate (see `03-ui-ux-principles.md` §9).

## 7. Image Processing Pipeline

### 7.1 From source to site

```
Source file (RAW / JPEG / PNG from photographer or stock provider)
  → Color correction if needed (Lightroom / Capture One)
  → Crop to target aspect ratio(s)
  → Export as high-quality JPEG to src/assets/
  → Astro <Image> component processes at build time:
    → Generates AVIF + WebP + JPEG at 400, 800, 1200, 1600px widths
    → Injects width/height attributes for CLS prevention
    → Outputs optimized files to the build directory
  → Browser receives the smallest appropriate file via <picture> + srcset
```

Never manually convert to WebP/AVIF and put optimized files in `public/`. Let Astro's build pipeline handle it so the process is repeatable and consistent.

### 7.2 Naming convention

Image source files in `src/assets/` follow this pattern:

```
[city]-[subject]-[context].jpg

detroit-dock-loading-trucks.jpg
detroit-warehouse-interior-wide.jpg
detroit-team-operations-meeting.jpg
detroit-skyline-from-facility.jpg
houston-fulfillment-picking-station.jpg
```

Descriptive, lowercase, hyphen-separated. The filename becomes part of the SEO signal (per `05-seo-aeo-geo-llmo.md` §2.6) — `detroit-warehouse-loading-dock.webp` is better than `IMG_4823.webp`.

### 7.3 Alt text standards

Every image gets alt text following the rules in `04-accessibility-wcag22aa.md` §3.1:

- **Informative images:** Describe what the image communicates, not just what it shows. "Forklift operator transferring pallets to an outbound trailer at the Detroit facility's east dock" — not "warehouse image."
- **Hero images:** Carry the emotional weight of the page. "Two warehouse workers coordinating a pallet transfer at dawn, with the Toledo skyline visible through the open dock door."
- **Decorative images:** `alt=""` — invisible to screen readers. Use only for pure texture/atmosphere images that add no informational content.
- **Include the city name** in alt text where the city is identifiable. This feeds local SEO signals.

## 8. Image Aspect Ratios by Context

Maintain consistent aspect ratios across the network:

| Context | Aspect Ratio | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Hero (full-bleed) | 16:9 | Landscape, full viewport width |
| Hero (contained) | 3:2 | When hero has text beside it, not behind it |
| Service card | 4:3 | Slightly taller than wide, good for card layouts |
| Blog post hero | 16:9 | Matches hero treatment for visual consistency |
| Blog in-body | 3:2 or 16:9 | Depends on content — wider for establishing shots, taller for details |
| Team headshot | 1:1 | Square, consistent across all team members |
| City character | 16:9 or 21:9 | Ultra-wide for cinematic city shots is allowed |
| OG share image | 1200×630 (≈1.9:1) | Fixed dimensions per social platform requirements |

Use CSS `aspect-ratio` with `object-fit: cover` to maintain these ratios regardless of the source image's native proportions:

```css
.hero-image {
  aspect-ratio: 16 / 9;
  object-fit: cover;
  object-position: center;
  width: 100%;
}
```

## 9. Image Color Treatment

### 9.1 No filters, no overlays by default

Photos are published at their natural color. No Instagram-style filters, no desaturation, no duotone, no color overlays. The warm industrial palette of real logistics photography already harmonizes with our brand colors — filtering it would fight the natural tone.

### 9.2 When a dark overlay is permitted

A semi-transparent dark overlay on a hero image is acceptable ONLY when text must be legible on top of the photo and the photo's background is too bright or busy:

```css
.hero-overlay {
  background: linear-gradient(
    to bottom,
    rgba(14, 26, 34, 0.5) 0%,    /* Midnight at 50% */
    rgba(14, 26, 34, 0.3) 100%   /* Lighter at bottom */
  );
}
```

Rules for overlays:
- Maximum opacity: 50%. Higher than this kills the photography.
- Use a gradient (darker at top where text lives, lighter at bottom) — never a flat overlay.
- The overlay color must be Midnight (`#0E1A22`) or Charcoal (`#1A1A1A`), never pure black.
- Text on the overlay must still pass WCAG AA contrast ratios.
- If the overlay makes the photo unrecognizable, choose a different photo that works without one.

### 9.3 Black-and-white treatment

A black-and-white version of a photo may be used for:
- Background texture at low opacity behind content sections.
- The 404 page's atmospheric treatment.
- A secondary image alongside a full-color primary (e.g., in a two-column layout where one image is color and one is B&W for visual contrast).

Never use B&W for the hero image. The hero is the emotional peak — it needs full color to earn its impact.

## 10. Icons and Illustrations

### 10.1 Icons

When icons are needed (service identifiers, process steps, UI indicators):
- Use a single icon set consistently across the site. **Lucide**, **Phosphor**, or **Heroicons** are approved — all are open-source, well-designed, and available as SVGs.
- Match icon stroke width to the body font weight (typically 1.5–2px stroke at 24px icon size).
- Color icons with brand palette colors only. Never use multi-colored icons.
- Icons are always supplementary — they appear alongside text, never as the sole identifier. An icon of a box means nothing without the word "Warehousing" next to it.

### 10.2 Illustrations

Custom illustrations are not part of the current Sams 3PL visual language. The brand is photographic, not illustrative. If illustrations are ever introduced:
- They must be commissioned, not generated.
- They must use the brand color palette.
- They must not replace photography on any page that requires it (per the non-negotiables).
- They are appropriate only for: process diagrams, data visualizations, and infographics where photography cannot convey the information.

### 10.3 Decorative SVG patterns

Subtle SVG patterns (dot grids, crosshatch, topographic lines) may be used as section backgrounds at very low opacity (5–10%) to add texture to otherwise solid-color sections. These must:
- Use brand colors only.
- Be invisible at a glance — they're felt, not seen.
- Never compete with photography or text.
- Be disabled under `prefers-reduced-motion` if they involve any animation.

## 11. Self-Check Before Shipping

1. Every page has at least one real photograph (non-negotiable #16).
2. No AI-generated images anywhere on the site (non-negotiable #15).
3. All stock photos come from the approved provider list (§2.2).
4. No stock photo triggers any of the "stock photo tells" from §4.2 (clipboard point, empty warehouse, handshake, overhead container, matching hard hats, lone truck, globe, over-saturation).
5. Every stock photo has been reverse-searched and appears on fewer than 5 competitor sites.
6. All images are processed through Astro's `<Image>` pipeline — AVIF/WebP/JPEG at 400/800/1200/1600px.
7. Hero images are under 150 KB at rendered size.
8. All images have descriptive, keyword-aware alt text with city names where identifiable.
9. All images have explicit `width` and `height` attributes (CLS prevention).
10. Image filenames are descriptive and hyphenated (`detroit-dock-loading.webp`, not `IMG_4823.webp`).
11. All licensed images are documented in the image license tracker.
12. All model and property releases are on file for commissioned photography.
13. No color filters or overlays are applied except where dark overlays are justified per §9.2.
14. Icons come from a single approved set and use brand colors only.
15. Aspect ratios match the context table in §8.
